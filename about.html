Learn More About Our Work And us

St. Francis S.S, Busunju is a brain child of the Capuchin Friars who set foot on Busunju land in the year 1989. Due to the challenges they observed in the area, they decided to start a school. For instance, the children from the primary schools in the area did not have schools to absorb them for secondary education. The only secondary school in the area was Ssekanyonyi secondary school which is 7 kilometres away from Busunju. As a result, many pupils dropped out of school and started doing petty businesses. The establishment of the school averted the situation. The school was spearheaded by Rev. Fr. Lawrence Dilraj Sequeira on behalf of the Capuchin Friars’ Fraternity. The school started in the structures of St. Joseph’s Busunju primary school and the head teacher, Mr. Lawrence Munyankindi, doubled as the head teacher of the secondary school. The staff, furniture, compound, name it, were shared. Just like any other starting project, the school experienced a terrible shortage of facilities like furniture, text-books, teachers, e.t.c. Children piled plunks of wood in order to get benches and where to write from. Some children carried chairs from home but when they reached school teachers took them away from them. When children brought books whose value they seemed not to know, the principal impounded them and stocked them in the school library. In 1992 the school started taking a more definite shape when it got its own head teacher, Rev. Fr. Pancratious Gerald Pinto. He served the school up to 2011 and went back to India only to be called by his Creator in 2014. May his soul rest in eternal peace! Since its inception, the school has transformed the socio-economic life of the people of Busunju and the surrounding areas. At present, the school gets children from all over the country. The school has bred professionals in different fields like teachers, politicians, the most reknowned of whom is Honourable Minister for tourism, Godfrey Kiwanda Ssuubi who was one of its pioneers in 1990.
